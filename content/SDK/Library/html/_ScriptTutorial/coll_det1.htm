Step One: Collision Detection and Boundaries

You can surround objects in a virtual world with an invisible boundary so that an avatar cannot pass through them - the object appears to be "solid." This technique is commonly referred to as adding "collision detection" to an object. Boundaries can also be passable, so that scripts can detect where an avatar or other object has intersected another object. This technique is useful in creating a "trip-wire" effect that allows scripts to detect where, and in what direction, an avatar or object is moving.

In this lesson, we move the camera outside of your avatar's position so that you can see your avatar in the third-person context. This will make it easier for you to see exactly when you cross the plane of an object. Your navigation will still work normally.
a Try moving your avatar with the arrow keys, out of the wire-frame box.
$ Notice what happens when you cross the boundary of the box, and notice that the star-like shape moves to the place where you crossed the boundary of the object.
a Click to place your cursor in the window, and then try moving your avatar out of the wire-frame box with the arrow keys.
a Look for the solid box nearby and navigate into it.
$ This boundary is not a trip-wire one, but has been made solid. The script it uses to detect a collision, however, is the same. In fact, both objects share exactly the same script from the same module, but only one will allow you to cross its boundary (the wire-frame box).
$ Thus, if you look at the Command Window reports for the detection scripts, you will notice that the solid object's script only reports collisions with the outer edge of its boundary, whereas the wire-frame can detect a user passing through it on either side.

Collision can be placed on any object at the top level of the Object Explorer hierarchy. For example, collision can be added to an object at the first level of a room's contents, and not within other objects. That is also why Lesson_6 needs to be two top level objects if there are two distinct objects with boundaries (check this out in the Object Explorer). Objects that are contained by other objects do not currently support collision detection.

In this example, each object has only one simple square boundary that surrounds it.
a You can verify this by looking at the objects from the boundary editor by clicking the Boundary Editor tab under the 3D view.
~ You lose your automatic third-person viewpoint when you go back to the normal view, but you can restart the lesson to regain it).
a If you feel comfortable with editing the boundary, you may try it to verify that the scripts still work.
$ A boundary does not have to correspond to an object's shape, and it does not even have to completely enclose its object. In addition, you can add as many boundaries to an object as you like, and the same script can handle all of them.

Step Three:

You can use impassable, or "solid," boundaries purely for design and navigation purposes, such as creating staircases and otherwise enhancing the illusion of three-dimensional space for users. Remember, however, that passable, trip-wire scripts can provide very useful information for potential interactivity, such as the number of users are in a certain place together at once. Another possible use might be to cause a user's movement in the world to change behaviors of objects. A trip-wire is a very easy technique to provide a way for objects to detect that a user is approaching or departing, in order to activate or deactivate a scripted behavior.
